01 / Why

By the time a problem is visible from the road, it has already cost you money.

Most crop trouble — disease, nutrient deficiency, water stress — starts in small patches nobody sees on foot or from a truck. By the time the whole field shows a sign, the patch has spread, the window to react has closed, and part of the yield is already gone.

The lag

A week of waiting costs real money.

Disease and stress don't pause between scouting visits. Every week without detection, more of the loss becomes permanent — even if the patch is treated later.

The blind spots

A ground crew can't cover a property of thousands of hectares.

Walking the field is slow. Looking from the headland doesn't reveal the centre of the field. By the time a patch is visible, it's usually 2 to 3 times larger than when it started.

The opportunity

Most of the loss is still recoverable — if caught in time.

In our test field, roughly 70% of the projected loss was still recoverable in week one. By week four, that share had already dropped sharply.

03 / Method

Why our drones find more, in less time.

Standard drone scouting

A drone that sweeps line by line, treating every square metre the same.

  • Burns flight time on the obviously healthy parts of the field
  • Misses early-stage patches that are still too small to notice
  • Long flights, more batteries, more pilot time, more cost
  • You get a pretty picture, not an answer
Our drone team

Drones that spend their battery where the trouble actually is.

  • Same patches found in ~40% less flight time
  • Or, in the same flight time, more patches found
  • Fewer batteries, less labour, lower cost per hectare
  • You get a short report telling you what to do

It's not about more drones. It's about smarter ones.

05 / Transparency

Where the dollar figures come from.

No black box. The loss numbers in the report follow the same logic your agronomist already knows — and are calibrated to your specific field.

The short version

Patch size × severity × crop price = potential loss. That's the internationally recognized damage-function approach used by university extension services and crop-protection networks. We then split that loss into "still recoverable" and "already permanent" using a recovery model that we calibrate to your own field over the first 1–2 flights.

— / The Honest Part

We haven't flown a field in Brazil yet. You should know that before anything else here.

The numbers above are real — they come from an independent academic dataset and a real US corn field. But they come from different soil, climate, and crop than yours. This document exists to present the technology honestly, not to oversell proof we don't yet have here.

What we have, and what we don't

Real technology. No local proof, yet.

The detection behind AlgoDroneTeams has been validated at 99.7% accuracy on a recognized academic benchmark, and the flight routing is roughly 40% more efficient than a standard sweep. Those are real, verifiable numbers — but they're not the same as a documented result on Mato Grosso soy or cotton, under tropical conditions, against the pests and diseases typical of the region (Asian soybean rust, stink bugs, cotton boll weevil). The loss-calculation methodology — area × severity × price — is the same one used globally; what still needs calibrating is the recovery model for your crop and your region.

09 / FAQ

A few of the questions you might have.

Do I need to learn any software?
No. We fly the drones, we process the images, we send you the report. You get a PDF, a map link, and a direct contact for any questions.
How long does a weekly flight take?
About 40 minutes of flight time for a field around the size of our test field (~95 hectares). The drones land themselves, and nothing on the ground has to stop or move.
What kind of "problems" can it actually spot?
Disease pockets, water stress, nutrient deficiency, and unusual growth patterns. Every patch comes with a best-guess cause and a severity score, so you and your agronomist can decide what to do.
How are the "potential loss" and "recoverable" numbers calculated?
The core — patch size × severity × crop price — is the standard damage-function approach used by agricultural extension institutions worldwide. Your agronomist will recognize the logic. The recoverable/permanent split uses our own recovery model, calibrated to your field over the first 1–2 flights. Model details available to your agronomist on request.
Has this been used on soy or cotton in Brazil?
Not yet. The results in this document come from an international academic dataset and a real US corn field. The detection and the loss-calculation methodology are the same across any crop, but the recovery model needs to be calibrated specifically for soy, cotton, and Mato Grosso's climate — something that only happens with real flights here.
What would a realistic starting point look like?
This document is informational only for now — there's no formal proposal here. If it's a fit for your operation, the natural next conversation would be designing, together, what a first field trial would look like.
