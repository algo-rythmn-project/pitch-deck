Reading the futures curve: a market-timing layer for crop decisions

Forward-curve analytics as decision support for crop marketing, storage, procurement and hedge-timing — designed to sit alongside field intelligence, not replace it.

17th International Conference on Precision Agriculture (ICPA) & 11th Brazilian Congress on Precision and Digital Agriculture (ConBAP) · Porto Alegre, Brazil · July 13–16, 2026

Remote sensing, yield models and soil data answer “what is happening in the field?” The futures curve answers a different question: “how is the market pricing timing, storage and supply risk?” We use the curve’s shape to identify the days when that market signal is unusually informative for a store-vs-sell decision.

The idea in one picture

A steep upward-sloping curve means the market is paying you to store grain and sell later; a flat or inverted curve means the market is telling you to sell now. Our model — a curve-shape factor model re-estimated as history accumulates, benchmarked against a random walk — predicts whether that storage reward will grow or shrink over the next month.

Act only on high-confidence days

The model does not treat every day equally. It ranks days by the size of the predicted move and only flags high-confidence days, when the curve is far from its historical shape and a larger adjustment is expected. These alerts are deliberately rare — for soybeans, roughly a handful of days per year (74 days across ~14 years of out-of-sample history): infrequent, but worth listening to.

Who is this for

Cooperatives & producers An alert that market timing risk is elevated — a prompt to review storage, sale timing or an open hedge, in plain language.
Grain buyers & processors A monitor for when deferred-vs-nearby pricing pressure is shifting — useful context for procurement and inventory decisions.
Agri-tech platforms A market-risk layer that drops into an existing advisory product alongside yield, weather and remote-sensing modules.
Researchers & agronomists A market-structure variable to test alongside agronomic data — not a replacement for crop knowledge, an addition to it.

Out-of-sample results across five crops

All results are from rolling out-of-sample validation: the model never sees the future when it predicts, and accuracy is always quoted against the all-days base rate — the edge is the gap, not the headline number. Crops where the method does not work are reported as such.

All experiments to date run on US and Canadian exchange data — CBOT futures (Chicago) for wheat, corn and soybeans, ICE Canada futures for canola. No Brazilian price series enters the models yet, by design: establishing how these signals transmit to Brazilian markets is the pilot’s job. Pairs with field intelligence — and we already run both layers

A companion satellite pipeline (MODIS + Google Earth Engine) estimates state/district-level yields across five crop-regions, including Brazil Mato Grosso soybean and corn. The monitoring layer works, and it is early where it matters: a safrinha-corn forecast lands months before any municipal official number in Brazil, and in the US and Canada the same pipeline runs ~4 weeks ahead of USDA corn and 36 days ahead of Statistics Canada on canola. Where official reporting is already fast — US wheat, and Brazilian soybean, which CONAB and IBGE cover monthly — we report no timing edge. The edge lives where official reporting is slow.

Part of what we bring is knowing, per crop, whether the satellite layer earns its keep or the yield trend alone suffices — a proprietary judgment, not a one-size-fits-all claim. For volatile, weather-driven crops (corn, canola) the satellite adds materially. For a stable, high-technology crop like Mato Grosso soybean, the yield is largely trend-predictable, so we deliver an accurate forecast without depending on the satellite. That distinction — where the field layer helps and where it does not — is exactly the kind of call that separates a real decision system from a dashboard.

The honest second finding: on the current small sample, the satellite yield signal has not yet added measurable price-prediction power beyond the curve signal alone. Quantifying that combination on Brazilian data is exactly the open research question this community is best placed to attack.

What we claim

  • The forward curve carries useful information about short-term crop-market timing risk.
  • The signal is strongest when conditioned on model confidence.
  • Soybean shows a robust, statistically significant real-time result.
  • Negative controls are built in — the framework rejects unsuitable markets.

What we don’t claim

  • That the method works on all crops — corn is ongoing research.
  • That it replaces agronomic knowledge or local judgment.
  • That CBOT/ICE results transfer one-for-one to Brazilian local prices — transmission to local basis, B3 contracts and BRL/USD is untested, and is precisely what a Brazil pilot would establish.

A Brazil pilot

  • Lead with soybean; SRW wheat as the large-sample benchmark; corn as a research track.
  • Test price transmission: CBOT signal vs B3 futures and local cash/basis series (e.g. Mato Grosso, Paraná), including BRL/USD effects.
  • Add Brazil-specific layers: regional yield outlook, weather stress, logistics, export basis, safrinha calendars — extending the existing satellite pipeline (already covering Mato Grosso corn) to soybeans.
  • Prototype a cooperative-facing dashboard: current signal, confidence level, historical analogues, crop-specific reliability label, plain-language context.
