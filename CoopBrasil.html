04 The Problem

Billion-dollar decisions,
made on Excel and tribal knowledge.

The co-op's biggest decisions — how much fertilizer to pre-buy, how to size grain positions, which members to extend credit to — are enterprise-level. But they only get smart when they're grounded in member-level truth. Today, that grounding is missing. The bridge is Excel and tribal knowledge.

  • › Member default events cluster in bad seasons — exactly when the co-op's own balance sheet is most exposed.
  • › Input procurement is locked months ahead based on aggregate guesses, not member-level demand signals.
  • › Aggregate trading positions are sized without knowing what 5,000 individual farms will actually deliver.
  • › Member-facing agronomy advice doesn't scale — and rival co-ops compete intensely on exactly this service.

06 What We Build
The product

Three models that feed up
from the member to the enterprise.

Each layer takes member-level signal as input and delivers an enterprise-level decision as output. That's the whole architecture.

01

Member-level default-risk → Credit Committee decisions

Per-member, year-ahead probability of repayment difficulty, built from credit history, plot-level satellite data, weather risk, and commodity exposure. The credit committee uses it to size and price each member's input financing.

02

Member-level input demand → Procurement & Trading positions

Member-by-member predictions of fertilizer, seed, and defensives demand — aggregated up so procurement negotiates better contracts, and the trading desk sizes its grain position knowing what 5,000 farms will actually deliver.

03

Conversational layer → Relationship Managers & (optional) Members

An agentic chat interface in Portuguese. Internal users — relationship managers, credit analysts — ask plain-language questions across the member base. Optionally, the co-op resells a member-facing version as a premium service.

12 Moats & Defensibility
Why this is hard to replicate

Enterprises see significant value.
Switching cost compounds with every safra.

This is not a prompt wrapper. Each of these barriers to entry deepens with time in production.

◆

Proprietary training data moat

Every co-op that goes live adds a new pool of labeled member-default and input-demand data. Models retrained on 3+ co-ops' data will outperform any new entrant without it — and the data is contractually exclusive to us.

◆

Grounding-without-hallucination framework

Two years of production hardening on messy Brazilian farm ERP data — credit records, plot registries, satellite imagery, logistics. Competitors starting from scratch face 12–18 months of the same grind.

◆

Deep ERP integrations

Co-op ERP systems (Totvs, SAP Agro, custom) are notoriously hard to integrate. Our data pipeline adapters are production-tested across multiple schemas — a year of work no one wants to repeat.

◆

Institutional stickiness

Credit committees and procurement desks rebuild workflows around our outputs. The co-op's operational processes depend on our forecasts. Switching cost is not technical — it's organizational and seasonal.

◆

Data sovereignty architecture

Models train and run inside the co-op's own cloud (AWS São Paulo or Azure Brazil South). Brazilian regulators are tightening on data residency. Our architecture is compliant by design.

◆

First-mover network in Paraná / Mato Grosso

Brazil's ag co-op sector is relationship-dense. The first vendor with a reference at Coamo or C.Vale gets warm introductions to every co-op in their orbit — and the second vendor gets resistance.
